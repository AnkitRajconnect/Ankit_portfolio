BeeAlp — A Full Market Entry
Strategy for Alpine Tourism

Helping a startup navigate the competitive alpine tourism space with rigorous positioning, a clear audience strategy, and an integrated go-to-market roadmap.

The Challenge

BeeAlp entered the alpine tourism market with a strong product concept but no clear brand positioning, no defined target audience, and no structured path to market. The founding team needed a complete strategic foundation — not just a marketing plan, but a coherent story about who they serve and why their experience matters.

The alpine tourism space is highly competitive, seasonal, and fragmented. Brands compete on price, location, and experience, often without a clear differentiating narrative. Our challenge was to find BeeAlp's genuine white space and build a strategy to own it.

Research Phase

Understanding the Landscape

The first step was a deep-dive competitive audit. I mapped over 15 comparable operators across Switzerland, Austria, and Northern Italy — analyzing their messaging, pricing, visual identity, customer reviews, and digital marketing footprint.

Three distinct audience segments emerged from the research: adventure-seekers aged 25–34 looking for authentic local experiences, families seeking structured outdoor activity with safety assurances, and eco-conscious travelers prioritizing sustainability credentials.

  • Competitive landscape analysis of 15+ alpine tourism operators
  • Customer persona development with behavioral and psychographic mapping
  • Keyword and content gap analysis across organic and paid channels
  • Review mining and sentiment analysis across TripAdvisor, Google, and Booking.com

Strategy

Positioning & Brand Architecture

The insight that shaped everything: most alpine tourism brands compete on the destination. BeeAlp's opportunity was to compete on the experience of being guided by locals who genuinely love where they live. This reframe — from "visit the Alps" to "belong to the Alps" — became the strategic foundation.

From this positioning, we developed a full brand architecture: a clear value proposition, a messaging hierarchy for each audience segment, tone of voice guidelines, and a visual direction rooted in warmth, authenticity, and the textures of the alpine environment.

Go-to-Market

Channel Strategy & Launch Roadmap

With positioning locked, I developed a 90-day launch roadmap across six channels — each mapped to specific audience segments and funnel stages. The strategy prioritized earned and organic channels in Phase 1 to build brand credibility before scaling paid acquisition in Phase 2.

  • SEO & Content: Long-form alpine travel guides targeting high-intent keywords, building organic authority ahead of peak season
  • Organic Social: Instagram and TikTok content strategy built around guide-hosted storytelling and user-generated content loops
  • Email: Segmented nurture sequences for each persona, tied to the booking consideration lifecycle
  • Partnerships: Outreach to eco-travel influencers, local tourism boards, and complementary outdoor brands
  • Paid Search: Tightly scoped Google Ads campaigns targeting high-commercial-intent queries in Phase 2
  • PR: Story pitches to travel and sustainability media, led by the "local expertise" narrative

Results & Outcome

What Was Delivered

The engagement concluded with a complete strategic deliverable package — positioning document, brand messaging guide, audience personas, 90-day channel roadmap with specific KPIs, and a content strategy brief for their first content team hire.

The founding team described the positioning framework as "the first time the brand truly felt like it had a point of view worth fighting for." The go-to-market roadmap was adopted in full for their upcoming pre-season launch.

Takeaways

What I Learned

This project reinforced that the most powerful marketing strategies often come from narrowing, not broadening. BeeAlp's instinct was to speak to everyone who loves the outdoors. The data told us to speak specifically to people who want to feel like they belong somewhere, not just pass through. That specificity is what gives campaigns something real to say.
